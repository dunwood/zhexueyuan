# 美伊战争第14天：伊朗为何仍能反击？美国是否已陷困境？

残局与僵局

美伊战争第14天：伊朗为何仍能反击？美国是否已陷困境？

2026年3月14日

2026年2月28日清晨，美以联军的炸弹划破德黑兰的天际——这场被美方代号为「史诗怒火」、以色列代号为「咆哮的狮子」的联合军事行动，正式拉开帷幕。14天后的今天，战争仍在燃烧，油价已突破每桶103美元，霍尔木兹海峡的通行量骤降97%，伊朗的导弹与无人机仍在向整个中东弧线上的目标呼啸而去。

这让很多人困惑：特朗普声称伊朗「没有海军、没有空军、没有通讯、导弹所剩无几」，那伊朗究竟凭什么继续打？更深层的问题是：世界头号军事强国美国，是否已在这场战争中遭遇了前所未有的战略困境？

一、伊朗真实军事实力：一支「反常规」的不对称战争机器

（一）数字背后的逻辑：不是传统大国，而是「威慑专家」

英国国际战略研究所（IISS）的《2025年军事平衡》报告显示，伊朗现役总兵力约61万人，其中正规陆军35万，伊斯兰革命卫队（IRGC）约19万。这组数字本身并不惊人——关键在于革命卫队掌握的是什么：导弹部队、无人机集群，以及整个「抵抗之弧」地区代理人网络的调度权。

更重要的是伊朗的军事哲学。数十年制裁封锁之下，伊朗被迫放弃与美以的常规军备竞赛，转而深耕两类武器：廉价可消耗的无人机蜂群，以及射程覆盖整个中东的弹道导弹。这不是无奈之举，而是一套精心设计的「不对称威慑」体系——让任何攻击者都必须在开战前计算代价，而这个代价将由全球能源市场来买单。

「我们的战略不是成为第二个美国，而是让美国成为不了第一个伊朗。」——伊朗国防部官员

（二）导弹库存：战时消耗后，伊朗还剩多少？

开战前，各方对伊朗导弹总库存的估算区间大致为2500—3000枚弹道/巡航导弹。根据战时开源情报的推算，截至2026年3月初，扣除已消耗弹药，伊朗可用库存仍约有1700—2100枚，其中弹道导弹约1000—1200枚，反舰导弹500—600枚。

此外，伊朗的战时月产能经评估可从平时的150—200枚提升至动员状态下的450—480枚/月——这意味着只要战争不在短时间内以决定性方式结束，伊朗的消耗可以得到持续补充。

在导弹光谱的顶端，是被外界格外关注的「法塔赫」系列高超音速导弹，飞行速度达13—15马赫，理论上可突破现役反导系统。但这类武器月产能仅约5枚，战时消耗极快，属于不可轻易动用的「战略储备牌」。

（三）无人机：数量、成本与蜂群战术的战略价值

伊朗在无人机领域的积累是这场战争中最令美军头疼的变量。就在开战前数周，伊朗陆军还正式列装了1000架新型无人机，涵盖飞翼隐身款、喷气式高速突防款和长翼展侦察/电子战款等多个型号。「目击者-136」系列的出口本身已证明了其战场价值——俄罗斯在乌克兰战场大量依赖该型无人机。

蜂群战术的核心价值不在于单架无人机有多致命，而在于它能以极低成本消耗极昂贵的拦截弹。一枚「目击者」无人机制造成本约2万美元，而美军用于拦截它的PAC-3 MSE导弹单价超过400万美元——这种「200:1」的成本不对称，在持续消耗战中对美国是结构性不利的。

（四）空军的「战略性缺席」：有意为之，而非全面溃败

特朗普声称「伊朗已没有空军」，此说虽有夸大，但部分属实。由于伊朗空军装备长期无法更新（主力仍是巴列维时代采购的F-14A，机龄超过50年），以色列已确认摧毁了包括假目标在内的全部已知F-14。但这场战争的事实已经反复证明：伊朗从未指望靠战机打赢这场仗。

伊朗的答案是：不用战机。从2024年至今，伊朗面对美以三次大规模空袭，均无战机起飞迎敌的可查记录。这是理性选择——保存有限资源，集中于有效反击手段。事实上，没有制空权并不妨碍伊朗实施战略级别的报复。

二、为何还能主动出击：「分散式」作战体系的韧性

（一）革命卫队的去中心化指挥

美以联军「斩首」行动最初的目标之一就是摧毁伊朗的指挥控制体系。然而，革命卫队的指挥架构并非金字塔式——它是高度分散的模块化结构，各作战单元具有相当程度的自主行动能力。这解释了为何在战争首日德黑兰指挥中枢遭到重创后，伊朗的反击依然迅速且有序：反应速度甚至比2025年「十二日战争」期间更快，外部分析认为这表明伊朗在那次战争后已对指挥架构进行了针对性的调整与加固。

（二）「导弹城」与地下工事：打不完的库存

伊朗多年来在全国各地修建了大量深埋地下的导弹储存与发射设施——被称为「导弹城」。美以联军虽已打击了50个弹道导弹库和革命卫队航空航天总部，但伊朗的地下设施分布广泛，部分洞库深度超过常规钻地弹的有效破坏深度。

据报道，开战以来伊朗的机动发射车从战前480部已损耗超过一半，降至约100—200部。但这仍足够维持分散突袭的作战节奏，而损失的发射车本身也是有意分散部署的「消耗品」。

（三）「抵抗之弧」：代理人网络作为战略纵深

伊朗单独的反击能力已经令人咋舌，而其战略纵深实际上远不止于此。黎巴嫩真主党已据报重建兵力至约10万人，并拥有2.5万枚各型导弹火箭弹和1000架无人机；也门胡塞武装持续对红海航运和以色列发动袭击；伊拉克民兵组织则对周边美军基地构成持续威胁。

这种「多线并发」的复合打击模式，迫使美以的拦截资源在多个战线被同时消耗，而每拦截一枚导弹或无人机的代价，都在消耗着美方本已紧张的拦截弹库存。

三、霍尔木兹海峡：不需要大海军也能「锁喉」世界

（一）地理决定命运：33公里宽的「核武器」

霍尔木兹海峡最窄处仅约33公里，深水航道仅3公里宽。每天约有2000万桶原油——占全球海运石油总量的20%—30%——必须经此通道输出。沙特、伊拉克、科威特、卡塔尔、阿联酋的油气出口，几乎全部依赖这条水道。

联合国贸发会议3月10日的数据显示，自冲突爆发以来，通过霍尔木兹海峡的船舶通行量已骤降97%——事实上的「封锁」已然实现，尽管伊朗从未在法律意义上正式宣布封锁。

（二）封锁不需要大海军：快艇、水雷与岸基导弹的「三位一体」

特朗普声称美军「摧毁了伊朗的海军」，这在常规舰艇层面可能接近事实。但革命卫队海军的核心从来就不是大型军舰——而是岸基反舰导弹、水雷、无人机和高速快艇蜂群。

布设水雷无需大型舰队：小艇可布，伪装商船可布，微型潜艇可秘密布设。据报道，伊朗已于3月11日开始在海峡布设水雷，估计目前数十枚规模，而其总储备约有6000枚。关键在于：即便只布几十枚雷，全球保险公司会立即将该水域评定为「战争水域」，商船自动暂停通行——封锁已经成功，甚至不需要击沉任何一艘船。

岸基系统方面，只要伊朗境内的反舰导弹阵地和岸防炮兵尚存一口气，3公里宽的深水航道就永远处于火力覆盖之内。这是一个极难从空中彻底摧毁的系统。

（三）「定向封锁」：伊朗的地缘政治工具

值得关注的是，伊朗的封锁并非无差别的——它是精心设计的地缘政治工具。伊朗明确表示，封锁仅针对「美国、以色列、欧洲及其盟友的船只」。据报道，能够通过海峡的船只「要么是中国所有，要么是伊朗的」，中国正与伊朗谈判，争取让中国原油船和卡塔尔液化天然气船安全通行。

这一策略的精妙之处在于：它既能最大化对美西方的经济打击，又能维系与中国这一最重要经济伙伴的关系，同时向国际社会传达「是美以制造了不安全」的叙事。

四、美国没招了吗？困境的真实结构

（一）拦截弹危机：拦不住的成本算术

这场战争中，美国面临的最严峻约束或许不是军事能力，而是弹药储备。开战仅100小时，冲突成本已达约37亿美元，其中35亿属计划外支出。仅拦截伊朗导弹和无人机，美军估计就已发射了价值约57亿美元的拦截弹。

2025年6月的「十二日战争」已经是预警：那场有限冲突消耗了美军20%的「标准」舰载防空导弹和50%的「萨德」拦截弹。这一次，战争规模更大、持续更长，五角大楼官员已在拟定追加预算计划，申请补充「爱国者」、「萨德」和「战斧」。

问题在于生产速度：美国PAC-3 MSE年产能约270枚，THAAD年产约37—40枚。在持续消耗战中，生产速度远追不上发射速度。

（二）政治目标的模糊性：轰炸能推翻政权吗？

特朗普的最终目标是「政权更迭」——让伊朗「好的领导人」接管。但美国智库卡托研究所专家的反问一针见血：「一次打击就能推翻政权吗？」历史告诉我们，对一个拥有复杂社会结构、深厚历史韧性和广泛地区影响力的国家，空袭不仅无法推翻政权，反而可能激发民族主义凝聚力。

更深层的困境是：美国并没有明确的「胜利终点」。地面入侵伊朗这一拥有8500万人口、面积相当于法德意三国总和的大国，在政治上完全不可行——特朗普已明确排除了这一选项。但仅靠空袭，无法实现政权更迭。

（三）国际孤立：中东盟友的暧昧与国际秩序的裂痕

这场战争正在制造一道深深的国际裂痕。美国著名经济学家杰弗里·萨克斯直言，此次军事行动是「1945年以来对《联合国宪章》最公然的违反」。法国总统马克龙呼吁安理会紧急开会，欧洲盟友整体态度审慎。

更重要的是，沙特——美国在中东最重要的传统盟友之一——曾明确表态「不允许其领空、领土被用于打击伊朗」。海湾国家在战场上已付出代价（科威特1死32伤、迪拜机场遭袭、巴林第五舰队总部被击中），却并非美以战争的主动参与者。这种被动卷入正在制造难以弥合的政治裂痕。

（四）美国并非「没招」，但所有「招」代价高昂

综合来看，美国并非走投无路——它仍拥有绝对的空中优势、更强的技术能力，以及在长期经济战中压垮伊朗的潜力。但在这场特殊的战争中，美国面对的是一个「不对称困境」：打赢常规意义上的空战轻而易举，但要实现「去军事化」乃至「政权更迭」的政治目标，则远超空中力量的能力边界。

伊朗已清醒地点明了它的战略：「打消耗战，摧毁美国乃至全球经济，削弱其所有军事能力至崩溃边缘。」这不是一个可以被轻易打败的对手——它的武器不是战机，而是时间、能源与全球供应链。

结语：不对称战争的本质教训

伊朗从未想过在传统战场上击败美国——它也不需要。它需要做到的，仅仅是让战争持续足够长的时间，让油价足够高、全球压力足够大，直到美国国内政治无法承受代价、国际社会介入斡旋，最终谈判桌取代战场。

这是一场关于「代价承受力」的战争，而非「军事实力」的战争。在这个维度上，伊朗的筹码比表面数字显示的要厚重得多——霍尔木兹海峡，本质上就是伊朗手中的「核威慑」：一旦动用，全球都要为此买单。

而美国面临的真正困境，或许不是军事上「没招了」，而是政治上「没有好结局」——无论是胜利还是陷入泥潭，代价都将以万亿美元和数十年地区秩序的重构来计量。

Stalemate and Endgame

Day 14 of the US-Iran War: Why Iran Can Still Strike Back — and Whether America Has Run Out of Options

March 14, 2026

On the morning of February 28, 2026, US and Israeli aircraft broke the silence over Tehran in an operation the Americans named "Epic Fury" and the Israelis called "Lion's Roar." Fourteen days later, the war continues to burn. Brent crude has breached $103 per barrel. Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has collapsed by 97 percent. Iranian missiles and drones are still streaking toward targets across the Middle East arc.

This raises a question that is baffling many observers: Donald Trump declared that Iran has "no navy, no communications, no air force, almost no missiles" — so how is Iran still fighting? The deeper question is whether the world's preeminent military power has stumbled into a strategic trap with no clean exit.

I. Iran's Real Military Capability: An Asymmetric War Machine

1. The Logic Behind the Numbers: Not a Conventional Power, but a Master of Deterrence

The IISS Military Balance 2025 puts Iran's active military at roughly 610,000 personnel: 350,000 in the regular army and 190,000 in the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC). The number itself is not remarkable — what matters is what the IRGC controls: the missile corps, drone fleets, and the command authority over an entire regional network of proxy forces.

More revealing is Iran's military philosophy. Decades of Western sanctions forced Iran to abandon any conventional arms race with the US and Israel. Instead, Tehran channeled its resources into two categories of weapons: cheap, expendable drone swarms and ballistic missiles capable of reaching every corner of the Middle East. This was not surrender — it was a deliberate design choice. The objective was to make any attacker calculate costs before striking, with those costs ultimately paid by global energy markets.

"Our strategy is not to become a second America — it is to make America unable to become a first Iran." — Iranian Defense Ministry official

2. The Missile Inventory: How Much Is Left After Two Weeks of War?

Pre-war estimates placed Iran's total ballistic and cruise missile inventory at 2,500—3,000 rounds. Open-source intelligence as of early March 2026 suggests the remaining usable stockpile stands at roughly 1,700—2,100 missiles, including 1,000—1,200 ballistic missiles and 500—600 anti-ship missiles.

Crucially, Iran's wartime production capacity is estimated to have scaled from a peacetime rate of 150—200 missiles per month to a mobilized output of 450—480 per month. As long as the conflict does not end decisively in the short term, production can partially offset consumption.

At the apex of the arsenal sits the Fattah hypersonic missile, flying at Mach 13—15, theoretically capable of penetrating existing missile defense systems. But its monthly production rate is only about five units — it is a finite strategic card, not a routine weapon.

3. Drones: Volume, Cost, and the Strategic Value of Swarm Tactics

Iran's drone buildup is the single most operationally disruptive factor for US forces in this war. Just weeks before the conflict began, the Iranian Army formally inducted 1,000 upgraded drones spanning multiple roles: flying-wing stealth designs, jet-powered high-speed penetrators, and long-endurance ISR/EW platforms. The export track record of the Shahed-136 — deployed extensively by Russia in Ukraine — has already demonstrated its battlefield value.

The strategic genius of swarm tactics is not the lethality of individual drones but the asymmetric cost exchange they impose. A single Shahed costs roughly $20,000 to produce. A PAC-3 MSE interceptor costs over $4 million. That 200-to-1 cost ratio, sustained over weeks of attritional warfare, imposes a structurally adverse arithmetic on the United States.

4. The 'Strategic Absence' of the Air Force: Deliberate Preservation, Not Collapse

Trump's claim that Iran has no air force is partially accurate but substantially misleading. Iran's air force — dominated by 1970s-era F-14As, F-4s, and aging MiG-29s — was never intended to contest air superiority in this conflict. Israel has confirmed the destruction of all known operational F-14s. Yet the war has repeatedly demonstrated that Iran never planned to fight with aircraft.

Through three major US-Israeli strikes since 2024, Iran has recorded no documented instances of fighter scrambles. This is rational resource conservation: preserve what little you have, concentrate firepower where it is effective. The absence of air power has not, in any meaningful sense, impaired Iran's capacity to execute strategic-level retaliation.

II. Why Iran Can Still Strike: The Resilience of a Decentralized System

1. The IRGC's Decentralized Command Architecture

One of the primary objectives of the US-Israeli "decapitation" strikes was to sever Iran's command-and-control infrastructure. Yet the IRGC's command architecture is not pyramidal — it is a highly modular, distributed structure in which individual operating units retain substantial autonomous authority. This explains why, even after Tehran's command nodes absorbed heavy strikes on the first day of the war, Iran's counter-attacks were rapid and organized. Analysts noted that Iran's response time was actually faster than during the 2025 Twelve-Day War — evidence that Tehran had drawn lessons and hardened its command architecture in the intervening months.

2. 'Missile Cities' and Underground Fortifications: A Stockpile That Cannot Be Fully Destroyed

Iran has spent years constructing a network of deep-underground missile storage and launch facilities, informally called "Missile Cities." US-Israeli forces have struck 50 missile depots and the IRGC aerospace headquarters. But Iran's subterranean infrastructure is geographically dispersed, and portions of it are buried at depths exceeding the effective penetration range of conventional bunker-buster munitions.

Iran's mobile launchers — estimated at around 480 pre-war — have been reduced by more than half, to perhaps 100—200 surviving vehicles. But that is still sufficient to sustain a dispersed strike tempo, and the lost launchers were intentionally designed to be expendable.

3. The 'Axis of Resistance': Proxy Networks as Strategic Depth

Iran's own counter-attack capacity is formidable. Its strategic depth is far greater still. Lebanese Hezbollah has reportedly rebuilt to approximately 100,000 fighters with 25,000 rockets and missiles and 1,000 drones. Houthi forces in Yemen continue to strike Red Sea shipping and Israeli territory. Iraqi militia groups maintain sustained pressure on US military facilities throughout the region.

This multi-front, composite strike model forces US-Israeli interceptor assets to be simultaneously consumed across multiple theaters. Every intercept — regardless of theater — draws down a common pool of interceptors whose replenishment rate is measured in months, not days.

III. The Strait of Hormuz: You Don't Need a Big Navy to Strangle the World

1. Geography as a Weapon: The 33-Kilometer 'Nuclear Option'

The Strait of Hormuz narrows to approximately 33 kilometers at its tightest point; the navigable deep-water channel is just 3 kilometers wide. Roughly 20 million barrels of oil per day — 20—30 percent of all seaborne oil — must transit this passage. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, and the UAE depend almost entirely on this corridor for their oil and gas exports.

UN Trade and Development data released on March 10 showed that vessel transits through the strait had collapsed by 97 percent since the conflict began. The blockade is functionally complete, without Iran ever having formally declared one.

2. Blockade Without a Navy: Mines, Shore-Based Missiles, and Fast-Boat Swarms

Trump declared that the US has destroyed Iran's navy. That is plausibly true for surface combatants. But the IRGC Navy's core assets were never large warships — they were shore-based anti-ship missiles, naval mines, armed drones, and high-speed fast-boat swarms.

Laying mines requires no large fleet: they can be deployed by small craft, disguised merchant vessels, or mini-submarines. Reports indicate Iran began seeding the strait with mines around March 11, with dozens deployed so far from an estimated reserve of approximately 6,000. The critical point: even a few dozen mines in a 3-kilometer channel are sufficient to cause global insurers to reclassify the waters as a war zone, at which point commercial shipping stops voluntarily. The blockade succeeds without sinking a single hull.

As long as any functional shore-based anti-ship battery survives on Iranian territory, a 3-kilometer shipping lane will remain under threat of fire. That system is extraordinarily difficult to permanently neutralize from the air.

3. Selective Blockade: Iran's Geopolitical Instrument

Notably, Iran's blockade is not indiscriminate — it is a carefully calibrated geopolitical tool. Tehran has explicitly restricted passage to vessels linked to the US, Israel, Europe, and their allies. Reports indicate that the ships currently transiting the strait are "either Chinese-owned or Iranian." Beijing is reportedly negotiating with Tehran to secure passage for Chinese crude carriers and Qatari LNG tankers.

The strategic elegance of this approach is that it maximizes economic damage to the Western-aligned world while preserving Iran's most important economic partnership, and projects the narrative internationally that the United States — not Iran — is responsible for the energy crisis.

IV. Has America Run Out of Options?

1. The Interceptor Crisis: An Arithmetic Problem

The most acute constraint the United States faces in this conflict may not be military capability but ammunition inventory. The first 100 hours of the conflict cost an estimated $3.7 billion, of which $3.5 billion was unplanned. The cost of intercepting Iranian missiles and drones alone is estimated at around $5.7 billion in expended interceptors.

The 2025 Twelve-Day War had already served as a warning: that limited conflict consumed 20 percent of the US Navy's Standard Missiles and 50 percent of THAAD interceptors in theater. This war is larger and longer. Pentagon officials are reportedly already drafting supplemental budget requests for additional Patriots, THAADs, and Tomahawks.

The production arithmetic is unforgiving: US annual output of PAC-3 MSE is approximately 270 rounds; THAAD production is 37—40 per year. In attritional warfare, production cannot pace consumption.

2. The Political Target: Can Bombing Topple a Government?

Trump's declared endgame is regime change — installing "good leaders" in Tehran. But a pointed question from the Cato Institute's Middle East expert Jon Hoffman cuts to the core: "Can one strike topple a regime? I don't think so." History suggests that aerial campaigns against countries with complex social structures, deep historical resilience, and broad regional influence tend to produce nationalist consolidation, not political collapse.

The deeper strategic problem is the absence of a defined endpoint. A ground invasion of Iran — a country of 85 million people covering an area comparable to France, Germany, and Italy combined — is politically unthinkable. Trump has already ruled it out. But airpower alone cannot achieve regime change. The strategic gap between declared objectives and available instruments is enormous.

3. International Isolation: The Fracturing of the US-Led Order

This war is opening deep fractures in the international order. Columbia University economist Jeffrey Sachs has called the operation "the most flagrant violation of the UN Charter since 1945." French President Macron demanded an emergency Security Council meeting. European allies have maintained studied ambiguity about the legitimacy of the campaign.

More consequentially, Saudi Arabia — arguably America's most important traditional partner in the region — had previously declared it would not allow its airspace or territory to be used against Iran. Gulf states have paid a steep price (Kuwait suffered casualties; Dubai's airport was struck; Bahrain's Fifth Fleet headquarters was hit) without being voluntary participants in the war. The political fractures this is opening in Washington's Gulf relationships will not heal quickly.

4. America Has Not Run Out of Options — But Every Option Carries a Steep Price

In sum, the United States is not helpless. It retains absolute air superiority, technological dominance, and the latent capacity to devastate Iran's economy over a prolonged campaign. But in this particular conflict, America faces an asymmetric dilemma: winning the air war in the conventional sense is straightforward; achieving the stated political objective of "de-militarization" or regime change lies far beyond what airpower can deliver.

Iran has stated its strategy with unusual candor: a long war of attrition that will "destroy the US and global economy and erode all of America's military capabilities to the breaking point." This is not a war aimed at defeating US weapons — it is a war aimed at defeating US political will, sustained by one of the most consequential geographic chokepoints on earth.

Conclusion: The Deeper Lesson of Asymmetric Warfare

Iran never intended to defeat the United States on a conventional battlefield — and it does not need to. Its strategic requirements are simpler and more achievable: sustain the conflict long enough, keep oil prices high enough, and generate sufficient international pressure until America's domestic politics can no longer absorb the cost and the world demands a negotiated settlement.

This is ultimately a war about cost tolerance, not military power. On that dimension, Iran's leverage is considerably more substantial than raw capability numbers suggest. The Strait of Hormuz functions, in essence, as Iran's nuclear deterrent: a geopolitical weapon so destructive to the global economy that its use forces the entire world to pay for Iran's survival.

The genuine strategic dilemma facing Washington may not be military at all — it may be that there is no good political outcome available. Whether the US wins, stalemates, or becomes mired in a prolonged conflict, the costs will be measured in the trillions of dollars and in decades of disruption to regional order.
