# 谈判桌上的炮声：美以为何选择在外交突破时刻开战？

> 发布日期: 2026-03-19

深度分析|国际事务

谈判桌上的炮声

美以为何选择在外交突破时刻开战？

2026年3月7日

![图片](/articles/images/谈判桌上的炮声：美以为何选择在外交突破时刻开战？/img_3.jpg)

2026年2月26日傍晚，阿曼外交大臣巴德尔·本·哈马德·布萨义迪走出日内瓦会议厅，向等候在外的记者们发表声明，回响随即传遍各国外交圈。他告诉记者，最新一轮美伊核谈判间接磋商已取得"显著进展"，双方已同意下周在维也纳继续技术层面的讨论。他暗示，一项协议似乎触手可及。

不到48小时后，德黑兰的夜空被爆炸声撕碎。

2026年2月28日，美国与以色列联合发动代号"史诗怒火"的军事行动，对伊朗核设施、导弹基础设施及军事领导层展开协调打击。3月1日凌晨，伊朗官方媒体证实了许多人最为担忧的消息：最高领袖阿里·哈梅内伊在此次袭击中身亡。谈判就此终结，战争随之而来。

"伊朗方面当时正试图富有创意地回应美方关切。而这一切，随着特朗普政府在谈判进行时选择军事打击，戛然而止。" ——美国外交关系委员会（CFR），2026年2月28日

困扰着政策制定者、法律学者与外交官的核心问题，并非这场战争在军事上是否可行——初轮打击的战果已给出了答案。真正的问题是：为何偏偏在外交看似奏效之时动手？答案令人不安，也指向国际谈判机制未来可能面临的深重危机。

一、一个外交时刻的解剖

要理解这场打击的时机选择，必须首先还原谈判桌上究竟发生了什么。与特朗普政府事后的说辞相反，此轮谈判并非毫无进展、无功而返。

自2月6日以来，在阿曼斡旋下，美伊双方已进行了三轮间接磋商。第一轮于马斯喀特举行，伊朗外长阿巴斯·阿拉格齐称其为"良好开端"。2月17日，第二轮谈判在日内瓦召开，据报双方在指导原则方面取得进展。至2月26日的第三轮谈判，阿曼调解方已宣布取得"显著进展"——包括伊朗在铀储量与核查机制上作出的若干让步。

就连美方谈判代表史蒂夫·威特科夫与贾里德·库什纳，也向特朗普表示，若假以时日，或许可以从伊朗那里争取到更多。美国外交关系委员会（CFR）高级研究员史蒂文·库克尖锐指出，伊朗已就暂停浓缩铀活动数年提出方案——这是一项有实质意义的让步，也是历届美国政府数年来孜孜以求的目标。

资料来源：美国外交关系委员会（CFR）、半岛电视台时间线、维基百科《2025—2026年伊美谈判》词条

2月25日，即最后一轮谈判的前一天，阿拉格齐在社交媒体上写道，避免冲突的"历史机遇""近在眼前"。阿曼外交大臣向哥伦比亚广播公司（CBS News）发出同样信号："我认为，除外交途径之外，任何其他选择都无法解决这一问题。"

不到24小时后，特朗普总统选择了"其他选择"。

二、"极限施压"的自我困局

批评人士指出，特朗普政府的做法存在一个根本性的内在矛盾：一方面高调推动外交斡旋，另一方面却在中东完成了自2003年伊拉克战争以来最大规模的美军部署。第二轮谈判期间，美国正将第二支航母打击群——杰拉尔德·福特号——调往该地区；与此同时，约二十架加油机已部署至以色列。美军中央司令部司令布拉德·库珀海军上将，正在日内瓦谈判进行时向白宫汇报对伊军事选项。

"当美国——尤其是在这届政府领导下——日后再度诉诸外交，将不得不面对巨大的信任赤字。" ——国际危机组织理查德·戈万

开战后数日内，特朗普政府对此次打击的公开理由几经变化，前后矛盾，漏洞百出。打击发动前，特朗普声称伊朗正在研发可打击美国本土的导弹——这一说法与美国国防情报局的评估严重不符，后者认为伊朗距离具备这一能力至少还有十年以上。国务卿鲁比奥将打击定性为应对"迫在眉睫的威胁"的必要回应，与五角大楼同周向国会所作简报的内容相互矛盾。国防部长赫格塞思则提出了第三套完全不同的理由。

资料来源：CNN政治频道，2026年3月3日——《特朗普的伊朗战争叙事：夸大威胁、目标游移、前后矛盾》

CNN援引消息人士及解密情报评估报道，特朗普及其官员在打击发动前"夸大了伊朗的能力"。半岛电视台则将此次事件与2003年伊拉克战争路数相提并论——流氓政权、迫切的核威胁、刻不容缓的时间压力——并指出"分析人士认为，美国政府正在翻出2003年的话术，为新一轮升级背书"。英国皇家联合服务研究所（RUSI）在打击发生后数小时内发布的快速反应评估中，措辞毫不含糊：伊朗"并无即将发动袭击的任何证据"。

三、外交是否从一开始就是幌子？

最具杀伤力的判断，并非来自对手，而是来自西方内部的法律学者、智库与前任外交官。他们正在追问的问题是：美方此番谈判，是否从一开始就出于真诚？

沃什伯恩大学法学院名誉教授、法律学者学会创始人阿里·汗，在一篇广为流传的《法学家》（JURIST）评论中写道，特朗普政府将"外交变成了一场精心策划的骗局"。他援引数百年来的契约法与条约法，引用西塞罗的名言——"正义的根基是诚信"——最终得出结论："欺骗在战场上或许合法有效，因为奇袭与谋略本是战争的应有之义。但战场上被容忍之事，不可移植至外交之中。"

资料来源：《法学家》（JURIST）法律评论，2026年3月6日

《波士顿环球报》的分析同样一针见血："美国与以色列在外交谈判正朝着正确方向推进之际发动了战争，并杀害了伊朗领导人……如果战争理应是穷尽一切外交努力后的最后手段，那么眼下发生的一切，显然不是这么回事。"

国际危机组织的理查德·戈万警告，这场冲突将给美国外交带来持久的"信任赤字"。他告诉《波士顿环球报》："我认为，现在人们会审视这一切，得出这样的判断：特朗普政府蓄意将外交当作烟幕弹和缓兵之计，一边吊着对方，一边备战。这意味着，未来与美国谈判将更加困难。"

四、欧洲的立场

欧洲各国首都对此事件的反应，从有条件支持到公开谴责不等，折射出当前西方联盟内部正在深化的裂痕。

在英国，首相基尔·斯塔默允许美军使用英国基地实施"防御性"打击，在国内引发轩然大波。大西洋理事会的欧洲简报指出，英国舆论的焦点集中于"美国的行动缺乏明确可实现的目标、国际法依据，也没有任何足以说明仓促开战的新证据"。

欧洲对外关系委员会（ECFR）的措辞则更为直白，其发布的评论文章题为《特朗普对伊朗的打击是非法的选择性战争——欧洲应当明确说出来》。查塔姆研究所认为，此次袭击是近期一系列事件中"进一步动摇全球秩序的重大一步"，并指出："这一秩序的核心原则是，任何国家不得在没有联合国安理会授权的情况下，为追求本国政策目标而诉诸战争。"

资料来源：大西洋理事会，2026年3月3日；查塔姆研究所，2026年3月；欧洲对外关系委员会，2026年2月28日

西班牙首相佩德罗·桑切斯明确表示反对。大西洋理事会评估认为，欧盟正身陷"结构性困境"——其以外交斡旋配合逐步施压为核心的对伊政策，如今已成明日黄花，而"连贯的新战略尚付阙如"。

五、军事打击能解决核问题吗？

抛开合法性与外交公信力的争议，仍有一个美国决策者最为在意的现实问题：这场战争，能奏效吗？

国际原子能机构（IAEA）于3月2日报告称，"没有迹象表明任何核设施遭到打击或损毁"——若属实，将直接动摇此次行动的核心军事逻辑。伊朗对此提出异议，声称一处设施已被击中。战争研究所评估认为，美以联合行动的重心在于压制防空系统、削弱伊朗的报复能力、瓦解其指挥控制体系——而这些目标与伊朗核计划本身并无直接关联。

"伊朗方面当时正试图富有创意地回应美方关切……若给予外交途径超过两周、多过两轮会谈的时间，或许可以从伊朗那里争取到更多。" ——CFR高级研究员史蒂文·库克

《对话》（The Conversation）的研究人员援引二十余年的核不扩散学术成果指出，"美伊双方公开划定的红线从根本上互不相容——这意味着谈判从一开始就注定难以成功"。但他们同时指出，谈判失败并不意味着战争就是合乎逻辑的下一步——尤其是当构成伊朗核能力的技术人才、浓缩铀储量与工程积累，根本无法通过空袭予以摧毁的时候。

历史先例已足够清晰。1981年，以色列对伊拉克奥西拉克核反应堆实施打击，萨达姆·侯赛因并未就此放弃核野心，反而将计划转入地下，加速推进。2007年，以色列对叙利亚疑似核设施发动袭击，结果同样如此。知识，是炸不毁的。

六、规则秩序的碎裂

超越伊朗本身，分析人士正在观察这场冲突向世界释放的信号。查塔姆研究所国际法项目负责人认为，此次美以打击，是近期一连串事件——包括俄罗斯入侵乌克兰、以色列2025年在停火谈判期间袭击卡塔尔，以及美国对格陵兰发出的威胁——之中的又一环，共同将以武力为常规政策工具的做法合法化、正常化。

"当前已有一种趋势，越来越多的国家将使用武力视为寻常的政策工具，"国际危机组织的戈万告诉《波士顿环球报》，"若安理会五常中有两个——俄罗斯与美国——同样如此行事，这向世界其他国家传递出一个信号：那道本已脆弱的反侵略禁忌，正在瓦解。"

大西洋理事会在梳理全球反应后发现，"美国与以色列联手对伊朗开战的形象，在中东地区各国政府与民众中观感极差"——而这种负面印象，远不止于中东。加拿大有条件地支持阻止伊朗获得核武器，但同时呼吁保护平民；法国和德国则在跨大西洋义务与自身对国际法的承诺之间左右为难，艰难权衡。

七、悬而未决的问题

3月1日，特朗普宣布美国接受伊朗提出的重启谈判的建议——这一举动本身表明，就连"史诗怒火"行动的发起者也清楚，炸弹无法单独解决这场危机。他随后表示，此次行动将在四周内结束。但终点是什么，终点之后又将如何，至今仍是一团迷雾。

大西洋理事会的罗布·麦凯尔或许一语道破了当下的处境："通往伊朗稳定的路，变得更窄了。"

历史将对2026年2月28日作出自己的裁决——那究竟是一场必要的先发制人，还是一次在外交最接近成功的时刻将其扼杀的不必要之战？对于这个问题，答案尚无定论。但有一点已无可争辩：在炸弹落下之前，阿曼外交大臣曾说，和平"近在眼前，只要给外交应有的空间"。炸弹没有留下那个空间。这场战争最终能带来什么，截至目前，仍是一个悬而未决的问题。

"伊朗官员完全有理由认为，外交不过是炮声响起前的一块幌子。" ——美国外交关系委员会（CFR）

**ANALYSIS|INTERNATIONAL AFFAIRS**

### Guns at the Negotiating Table

Why the US and Israel Chose War at the Moment of Diplomatic Breakthrough

March 7, 2026

On the evening of February 26, 2026, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr bin Hamad Al Busaidi walked out of a Geneva conference room and made a statement that reverberated across diplomatic capitals. The latest round of indirect US-Iran nuclear talks, he told reporters, had achieved "significant progress." Both sides had agreed to continue technical-level discussions in Vienna the following week. A deal, he suggested, was within reach.

Less than 48 hours later, the skies over Tehran lit up with explosions.

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury — a coordinated assault targeting Iran's nuclear facilities, missile infrastructure, and military leadership. By the early hours of March 1, Iranian state media confirmed what many had feared: Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei had been killed. The talks were over. The war had begun.

"The Iranian side was trying to be imaginative in addressing U.S. concerns. All this has now ended, as the Trump administration opted for military attacks while the talks were unfolding." — Council on Foreign Relations, February 28, 2026

The central question haunting policymakers, legal scholars, and diplomats is not whether the war was militarily feasible — the early strikes made that clear enough. The question is why it happened precisely when diplomacy appeared to be working. The answer reveals something deeply troubling about the future of international negotiation.

### I. The Anatomy of a Diplomatic Moment

To understand the timing of the strikes, it helps to reconstruct what was actually happening at the negotiating table. Contrary to what the Trump administration later suggested, the talks were not going nowhere.

Three rounds of indirect negotiations had taken place since February 6, all mediated by Oman. The first session in Muscat was described by Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi as a "good start." The second round, held February 17 in Geneva, resulted in reported progress on guiding principles. By the third round on February 26, the Omani mediator was speaking of "significant advances" — including Iranian concessions on uranium stockpiling and verification.

Even Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, the US negotiating team, acknowledged to Trump that more could potentially be extracted from Iran given more time. The CFR's Steven Cook noted pointedly that Iran had tabled proposals calling for a suspension of uranium enrichment for several years — a meaningful concession that previous administrations had pursued for years.

Sources: Council on Foreign Relations (CFR), Al Jazeera timeline, Wikipedia/2025-2026 Iran-US Negotiations

On February 25, a day before the final round of talks, Araghchi posted on social media that a "historic opportunity" to avert conflict was "within reach." The Omani foreign minister echoed that sentiment to CBS News: "I don't think any alternative to diplomacy is going to solve this problem."

Less than 24 hours later, President Trump chose the alternative.

### II. The "Maximum Pressure" Trap

Critics have pointed to a structural contradiction in the Trump approach. The administration publicly committed to diplomacy while simultaneously mounting the largest US military buildup in the Middle East since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. A second aircraft carrier, the USS Gerald R. Ford, was deployed to the region the same week as the second round of negotiations. Twenty tanker aircraft were positioned in Israel. CENTCOM commander Admiral Brad Cooper was briefing the White House on military options while the Geneva talks were ongoing.

"When the US — certainly under this administration — engages in diplomacy down the road, it is just going to suffer from an enormous trust deficit." — Richard Gowan, International Crisis Group

The Trump administration's public justifications for the strikes shifted repeatedly and contradictorily in the days that followed. Before the attack, Trump claimed Iran was developing missiles capable of reaching the United States — a claim not supported by US Defense Intelligence Agency assessments, which placed that capability more than a decade away. Secretary of State Rubio argued the strikes were a response to an "imminent threat" — contradicting Pentagon briefings given to Congress the same week. Defense Secretary Hegseth outlined a third set of rationale entirely.

Source: CNN Politics, March 3, 2026 — "Trump's Iran war message marked by exaggerated threats and shifting, contradictory goals"

CNN reported that Trump and his officials "overstated Iran's capabilities" in the lead-up to strikes, according to sources and unclassified intelligence assessments. Al Jazeera drew explicit parallels to the 2003 Iraq playbook — a rogue regime, an urgent nuclear threat, a ticking clock — noting that "analysts say the US administration is recycling the rhetoric of 2003 to justify a new escalation."

The Royal United Services Institute (RUSI), in its rapid reaction assessment published hours after the strikes, was unambiguous: there was "no evidence of an imminent attack by Iran."

### III. Was Diplomacy Ever Real?

The most damaging verdict comes not from adversaries but from legal scholars, think tanks, and former diplomats in the West. The question they are asking is whether the negotiations were conducted in good faith at all.

Ali Khan, Emeritus Professor of Law at Washburn University School of Law and founder of Legal Scholar Academy, argued in a widely-read JURIST commentary that the Trump administration transformed "diplomacy into a calculated ruse." Drawing on centuries of contract and treaty law — citing Cicero's observation that "the foundation of justice is good faith" — he concluded that "deception may be a lawful and effective instrument on the battlefield, where surprise and stratagem are expected. But what is tolerated in combat cannot be imported into diplomacy."

Source: JURIST Legal Commentary, March 6, 2026

The Boston Globe's analysis was equally stark: "The United States and Israel started a war and killed Iran's leadership while they were in the middle of diplomatic negotiations that were reportedly going in the right direction... If war is supposed to be the last resort after all diplomatic efforts fail, that's clearly not what happened here."

Richard Gowan of the International Crisis Group warned that this conflict would create a lasting "trust deficit" for American diplomacy. "I think now people will look at this and say it looks like the Trump administration deliberately uses diplomacy as a distraction and as a tool to keep people dangling while preparing for war," Gowan told the Globe. "And I think that does mean that it will be harder for people to negotiate with the US in the future."

### IV. The View from Europe

The reaction across European capitals has ranged from conditional support to open condemnation, reflecting the fault lines now running through the Western alliance.

In the United Kingdom, Prime Minister Keir Starmer allowed US forces to use British bases for "defensive" strikes — triggering a political firestorm at home. The Atlantic Council's Europe dispatch noted that British public opinion was dominated by "the United States' lack of clear, realizable objectives, a legal basis under international law, or new evidence to justify the rush to war."

The European Council on Foreign Relations put it more bluntly, publishing a piece titled "Trump's strikes on Iran are an illegal war of choice — and Europeans should say so." Chatham House argued the attacks represented "a further, major step in unhinging the global order," noting that "the core principle of that order is that no state can go to war in pursuit of its own national policy" without a UN Security Council mandate.

Sources: Atlantic Council, March 3, 2026; Chatham House, March 2026; European Council on Foreign Relations, February 28, 2026

Spain's Prime Minister Pedro Sanchez voiced outright opposition. The European Union found itself, in the Atlantic Council's assessment, in "a structurally uncomfortable position" — its Iran policy "anchored in diplomacy backed by incremental pressure" now rendered obsolete, with "a coherent new strategy yet to emerge."

### V. Can Military Force Solve the Nuclear Problem?

Setting aside questions of legality and diplomatic credibility, there remains the practical question that American policymakers care most about: will this work?

The IAEA reported on March 2 that it had "no indication that any of the nuclear installations had been hit or damaged in the strikes" — a finding that, if accurate, would undermine the core military rationale. Iran contested the assessment, claiming one site had been struck. The Institute for the Study of War assessed that US-Israeli operations had focused on suppressing air defences, degrading Iran's retaliatory capacity, and disrupting command and control — objectives that say little about the actual nuclear program.

"The Iranian side was trying to be imaginative... Perhaps more could have been extracted from Iran if diplomacy had more time than a mere two weeks and two sessions." — CFR Senior Fellow Steven A. Cook

Researchers at The Conversation, drawing on two decades of nonproliferation scholarship, noted that "publicly stated red lines by both sides were incompatible with each other — meaning negotiations were always likely to fail." But failure of negotiations, they argued, does not make war the logical next step — particularly when the technical knowledge, enriched uranium stockpiles, and engineering expertise that constitute Iran's nuclear capability cannot be destroyed by airstrikes.

The historical precedent is clear. Israel's 1981 strike on Iraq's Osirak reactor did not end Saddam Hussein's nuclear ambitions — it accelerated them, driving the program underground. The 2007 strikes on Syria's alleged nuclear facility similarly failed to eliminate the underlying intent. Knowledge cannot be bombed away.

### VI. The Fracturing of the Rules-Based Order

Beyond Iran, analysts are watching what this conflict signals to the rest of the world. Chatham House's international law program director argued that the US-Israeli strikes represent one in a series of recent events — including Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Israel's strike on Qatar during ceasefire negotiations in 2025, and US threats over Greenland — that are collectively normalizing the use of force as a routine policy instrument.

"There's already a trend towards countries using force as a more normal policy tool," Gowan of the International Crisis Group told the Boston Globe. "And if you see two of the five permanent members of the Security Council doing the same — Russia and the US — that sends a message to pretty much everyone else that the always-fragile taboo against aggression is fragmenting."

The Atlantic Council, summarizing global reactions, found that "the optics of the United States and Israel jointly going to war in Iran are not sitting well with countries and the public in the region" — and beyond. Canada expressed conditional support for denying Iran nuclear weapons while calling for civilian protection; France and Germany find themselves navigating between transatlantic solidarity and their own commitment to international law.

### VII. The Question That Remains

On March 1, Trump announced that the US had accepted an Iranian proposal to resume negotiations — a move that suggested even the architects of Operation Epic Fury understood that bombs alone could not resolve this crisis. He later said the operation would be completed within a four-week timetable. The endpoint, and what comes after, remains deeply unclear.

The Atlantic Council's Rob Macaire perhaps summarized the situation most succinctly: "The pathway to a stable Iran just got narrower."

History will render its own judgment on whether February 28, 2026 was a necessary act of preemption or an unnecessary act of aggression that killed diplomacy at the moment it was closest to succeeding. What is already beyond dispute is what Oman's foreign minister said before the bombs fell — that peace was "within reach, if we just allow diplomacy the space it needs to get there." The bombs did not allow that space. Whether the resulting war produces anything better remains, as of now, an open question.
