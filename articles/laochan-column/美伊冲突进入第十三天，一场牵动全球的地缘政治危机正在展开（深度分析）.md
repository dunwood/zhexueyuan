一、经济冲击：霍尔木兹海峡的代价

能源市场的系统性震荡

自2月28日美以联合发动「史诗怒火行动」以来，全球能源市场经历了近半个世纪以来最剧烈的结构性冲击。

霍尔木兹海峡是全球石油贸易的咽喉要道，平日承载全球约五分之一的石油与液化天然气运输。战争爆发后，伊朗宣布封锁这一水道，伊拉克、科威特已率先削减产量，阿联酋、沙特阿拉伯亦面临相同压力。国际能源署评估，因无储存空间，中东产油国已被迫共计减产至少每日1000万桶——这是人类历史上规模最大的单次石油供应中断。

国际油价在两周内剧烈震荡：布伦特原油期货一度冲至每桶119.5美元的四年高点，随后因IEA宣布创纪录动用4亿桶战略石油储备而大幅回落，但市场情绪依然极度脆弱。分析师警告，若局势持续，油价存在向每桶150美元冲击的可能，届时将触发发展中国家的「需求破坏」效应——即高油价将强制压制消费。

每上涨10%的油价，将拉升全球通胀约0.4个百分点，并削减全球经济增长约0.15个百分点。——国际货币基金组织

对美国而言，汽油均价已升至每加仑3.41美元，加州超过5美元。高盛预计，若油价维持高位数月，美国全年CPI或从1月的2.4%攀升至3%，从根本上封锁了联储降息的空间。这与特朗普政府的关税政策共同形成「双重通胀压力」，令经济政策陷入两难。

对亚洲的冲击尤为深远。流经霍尔木兹的原油和液化天然气中约80%至90%输往亚洲，中国、日本、韩国、印度均高度依赖这一通道。印度更已有逾40万吨出口大米滞留港口，因战争打乱了中东航线。瑞银、野村等机构预计，此次冲击将对亚洲CPI产生7至27个基点的直接拉动，泰国、韩国、新加坡受影响最深。

供应链连锁反应

战争的代价远不止能源价格。伊朗对迪拜杰贝阿里港的打击令全球第九大港口一度陷入瘫痪；多国关闭领空，英国航空、汉莎、国泰等主要航司暂停中东航线；硫磺、氦气等工业关键原料——前者供应全球45%来自海湾地区，后者对半导体制造至关重要——均面临严重短缺，可能进一步推高芯片及化肥价格。

伦巴德奥迪尔资产管理公司的情景分析指出，在持久冲突的风险情景下，美国2026年实际GDP增速可能降至1.2%，欧元区则将面临能源依赖带来的双重打击：油价上涨叠加美国关税压力，令欧洲央行陷入「真正的政策两难」——既需应对通胀，又要支撑疲弱的增长。

二、战争走向：一场没有退出战略的战争

战场形势：军事上胜，政治上困

美以联军在军事行动上取得了显著进展，但政治目标的模糊性正令这场战争走向一个令人忧虑的未知地带。

战争爆发两周内，美以联军对伊朗发动近2000次打击，摧毁了核设施、弹道导弹基础设施及多处高层领导人据点，最高领袖哈梅内伊在首轮打击中身亡。伊朗现任最高领袖已由其子穆杰塔巴·哈梅内伊接任，但伊斯兰革命卫队核心结构并未瓦解，反而在新领袖就位后迅速宣誓效忠。

伊朗则以导弹和无人机对以色列、美军驻海湾基地、约旦、科威特、卡塔尔等地展开多轮报复打击，并威胁在轰炸停止前不允许「一滴石油」通过霍尔木兹海峡。伊朗高层官员阿里·拉里贾尼的公开表态直接回应了特朗普的威胁：「比你们更强大的人都无法消灭伊朗。你们自己当心别被消灭。」

军事手段已被授权超出战略目标所能兑现的范围。美军可以摧毁伊朗的硬件，却无法从空中制造一个政治替代方案。——大西洋理事会分析师塞卢姆

目前，战争是否会持续，在很大程度上取决于三个变量：其一，伊朗政权的韧性。分析人士普遍指出，尽管失去哈梅内伊，伊朗的体制韧性远超外界预期，新最高领袖已迅速完成了权力传承；其二，美国是否进行地面入侵。一旦地面部队介入，战争性质将根本改变，伤亡与泥潭化风险将急剧上升；其三，停火谈判的成效。中国、俄罗斯、法国已分别就停火与伊朗接触，伊朗方面则坚持以「停止军事行动」为谈判前提条件。

停火窗口：存在但脆弱

目前存在一个微弱但真实的停火窗口。特朗普于3月10日罕见召开新闻发布会，暗示美国目标「基本已实现」，并称冲突「可能比预期更快结束」。然而，这与国防部长赫格塞斯同日表示战争是「开始、中间还是结束」由特朗普决定的说辞并不一致，显示出白宫内部的信息混乱。

更深层的障碍在于：伊朗内部并未出现预期中的政权崩溃，新最高领袖的就位反而为政权提供了一定的合法性背书；美国若接受以伊斯兰革命卫队主导的过渡政府——类似于其在委内瑞拉所采取的替换模式——则面临「可能安装一个更极端权力核心」的风险，这是特朗普自己都承认的「最坏情境」。

三、美国深陷泥潭的风险

历史的幽灵：从伊拉克到伊朗

批评者正在援引2003年伊拉克的幽灵。但支持者认为，这场战争从本质上不同于那次陆地占领——真正的问题是，特朗普是否有能力在军事胜利与政治目标之间找到出口。

从军事部署规模看，美军目前动用了两个航母战斗群及16艘其他水面作战舰艇，约占海军总战力的40%。这是自2003年伊拉克战争以来美军在中东最大规模的投入。目前已有7名美军士兵阵亡，联合参谋长主席卡因上将警告公众「做好更多伤亡的心理准备」。

大西洋理事会战略分析师梅拉妮·哈特指出，此次中东冲突已迫使美军将部分最先进的导弹防御系统从印太战区转移至中东，「这对北京来说是一次重大战略红利」。若美国最终陷入另一场中东泥潭，印太方向的战略重心将被严重稀释，中国将从中获得极大的战略空间。

他们没有计划。他们没有时间表。因此，他们也没有退出战略。——美国参议员马克·凯利（民主党，亚利桑那州）

国内政治层面，民调显示约六成美国成年人表示不信任特朗普在伊朗动武问题上的决策。特朗普原本以「结束永久战争」为竞选核心承诺，但他对伊朗战争的反复定性——在同一场演讲中先称「短期探险」再称「赢得还不够」——令其核心选民开始产生困惑与不满。油价上涨、通胀压力以及潜在的更多伤亡，构成了2026年中期选举前的重大政治风险。

然而，也有另一种可能性不应被忽视：若美以行动成功摧毁伊朗的核能力与弹道导弹体系，同时促成一个相对温和的政权更替，特朗普完全可能以「结束近50年美伊对峙」的姿态获得历史性的外交红利。这取决于两个条件：战争能否在不引入地面部队的情况下终结，以及伊朗内部的政治走向。

四、国际外交：裂痕中的博弈

多极世界的压力测试

这场战争是后冷战秩序最严峻的一次压力测试，正在沿着可预见的轴线重新划定国际阵营。

在反对阵营中，中俄两国在联合国安理会紧急会议上共同要求立即停火，并谴责美以行动违反《联合国宪章》。但观察人士指出，两国的立场存在内在矛盾：俄罗斯长期反对外部干涉，却本身在乌克兰进行同性质的军事行动；中国则在强烈谴责之外，刻意保持了「不提供直接军事支持」的克制。

中国的战略尤为值得关注。北京购买了伊朗约80%的石油出口，但这仅占中国总进口量的不足15%。多年来，中国一直在为「马六甲困境」寻找替代方案，以应对美国可能切断中东能源供应的风险。专家普遍认为北京仍优先维护对美关系。

在支持阵营中，北约秘书长马克·吕特称欧洲「支持」美国的行动，德国总理弗里德里希·默茨则持更为审慎的立场：他公开表示「每过一天，出现的问题就越多」，并强调欧洲「没有兴趣看到一场没有尽头的战争」，更不希望看到伊朗领土的瓦解。英国、法国表态支持「防御性军事措施」以应对无人机和弹道导弹威胁，但均不愿深度卷入。

土耳其的关键角色

土耳其是目前唯一在两方之间具有实质斡旋能力的区域大国。土耳其总统埃尔多安是少数公开谴责「双方行为」的领导人，同时强调安卡拉正在进行「密集的外交努力」。土耳其的特殊性在于：它是北约成员国，同时与伊朗保持传统外交联系，且其领土是目前伊朗唯一未被列入打击目标的周边大国。

另一个值得关注的变量是以色列在黎巴嫩开辟的第二战场。以军于3月3日授权对黎巴嫩进行地面入侵，进一步打击真主党基础设施，这意味着战争正在从伊朗向整个「抵抗轴心」扩散，大幅增加了局势失控的风险。

结语

不确定性中的确定性

这场战争的走向，将由以下几个核心问题的答案所决定。

第一，特朗普是否会在军事目标「基本实现」后接受一个并不完美的政治结局——即一个仍由伊斯兰革命卫队主导、但已无力挑战地区秩序的伊朗？这是目前最有可能的停战出口，但需要特朗普在国内政治上付出一定代价。

第二，伊朗能否维持足够的战略韧性以争取体面的停火条件？新最高领袖穆杰塔巴·哈梅内伊面对的是一个被严重削弱但尚未崩溃的体制，其政策走向尚难预判。

第三，霍尔木兹海峡的封锁能否在数周内得到缓解？这是关乎全球经济稳定最紧迫的问题。IEA史无前例地动用4亿桶战略储备，本身就是一个强烈信号：这场战争比任何人预期的都要持久。

可以确定的是：无论战争走向如何，中东地缘政治版图、全球能源架构与国际多边秩序都将因这场冲突而发生深刻改变。当下的每一天，都在重写历史。

IN-DEPTH ANALYSIS

The Middle East on Fire: Economic Shock, War's Trajectory, and the Global Diplomatic Fracture

As the U.S.-Iran war enters its 13th day, a geopolitical crisis of global consequence unfolds

March 13, 2026

I. ECONOMIC SHOCK: THE PRICE OF HORMUZ

A Structural Blow to Global Energy Markets

Since the joint U.S.-Israeli launch of 'Operation Epic Fury' on February 28, global energy markets have absorbed a structural shock not seen in nearly half a century.

The Strait of Hormuz — the world's most critical energy chokepoint — ordinarily channels roughly one-fifth of global oil and LNG supplies. Since Iran's effective closure of the waterway, Iraq and Kuwait have already been forced to cut production, with the UAE and Saudi Arabia facing similar pressures as tankers queue with nowhere to offload. The International Energy Agency has assessed that Gulf producers have collectively reduced output by at least 10 million barrels per day — the largest single supply disruption in history.

Oil prices swung violently: Brent crude briefly touched $119.50 per barrel — a four-year high — before retreating sharply after the IEA announced a record coordinated drawdown of 400 million barrels from strategic reserves. The U.S. separately tapped 172 million barrels from its Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Yet markets remain deeply unsettled. Analysts warn that if physical shortfalls materialize, prices may need to surge to $120–150 per barrel to force demand destruction in developing economies.

Every 10% rise in oil prices corresponds to a 0.4% rise in inflation and a 0.15% reduction in economic growth globally. — International Monetary Fund

For American consumers, the average pump price has already risen to $3.41 per gallon nationally — above $5 in California. Goldman Sachs estimates that sustained elevated oil prices could push U.S. headline CPI from 2.4% in January to 3% by year-end, effectively locking the Federal Reserve out of rate-cutting cycles for 2026. This compounds the inflationary pressure of Trump's tariffs, creating a dual-front economic squeeze.

Asia faces the most acute exposure. Roughly 80–90% of crude and LNG transiting Hormuz is destined for the region. China, Japan, South Korea, and India are all critically reliant on this corridor. India is already feeling the pain: over 400,000 metric tons of basmati rice designated for export are stranded at Indian ports as the war disrupts Middle Eastern shipping lanes. Fitch Solutions estimates the conflict will add 7–27 basis points to Asian CPI, with Thailand, South Korea, and Singapore most exposed.

Supply Chain Contagion

The economic fallout extends far beyond oil. Iranian strikes on Dubai's Jebel Ali port — the world's ninth-largest — have disrupted regional logistics. Major airlines including British Airways, Lufthansa, and Cathay Pacific have suspended Middle Eastern routes as airspace closures multiply. Sulfur, of which Gulf states supply 45% of global volumes, and helium, critical to semiconductor manufacturing, both face acute shortages with ripple effects for fertilizers, chipmakers, and industrial producers.

Lombard Odier's scenario analysis projects that in a prolonged conflict, U.S. real GDP growth could slow to 1.2% in 2026, while the eurozone faces a dual shock of energy price inflation and weakened trade conditions — placing the European Central Bank in what ING economists call a 'genuine dilemma' between fighting inflation and supporting growth.

II. WAR'S TRAJECTORY: A CONFLICT WITHOUT AN EXIT

Military Gains, Political Paralysis

The U.S.-Israeli military campaign has delivered significant tactical results. But the absence of a coherent political endgame is pushing the war toward unpredictable territory.

In two weeks of combat, U.S. and Israeli forces have struck nearly 2,000 targets inside Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in the opening salvo and severely degrading Iran's nuclear infrastructure, ballistic missile program, and naval assets. Both countries now claim control of Iranian airspace. Yet the Islamic Republic has not collapsed. Iran's Revolutionary Guard moved swiftly to consolidate behind Khamenei's successor — his 56-year-old son Mojtaba Khamenei, elected Supreme Leader on March 8 — and senior officials including Ghalibaf, Larijani, and President Pezeshkian pledged immediate allegiance.

Iran's retaliatory posture has been expansive: missile and drone strikes have targeted Israel, U.S. bases in Qatar, the UAE, Bahrain, and Jordan, as well as oil tankers in the Gulf of Oman. The Revolutionary Guard has vowed that not a 'single liter of oil' will transit Hormuz until the bombing stops. Senior official Ali Larijani responded directly to Trump's threats: 'The sacrificial nation of Iran doesn't fear your empty threats. Even those bigger than you couldn't eliminate Iran.'

The military instrument has been authorized far beyond what the strategic objective can deliver. The U.S. can destroy Iran's hardware, but it cannot manufacture a political alternative from the air. — Atlantic Council analyst Seloom

Whether the war continues hinges on three variables: first, the resilience of Iran's political structure, which has proved considerably more durable than anticipated; second, whether the U.S. commits ground troops, a step that would fundamentally alter the conflict's character and risks; and third, the viability of ceasefire diplomacy, to which China, Russia, and France have all signaled openness — though Iran insists on a cessation of attacks as a precondition.

A Fragile Ceasefire Window

A narrow but real window for cessation exists. Trump held a rare press conference on March 10 suggesting U.S. objectives had been 'largely met' and that the conflict might end 'sooner than expected.' Yet this coexists uncomfortably with Defense Secretary Hegseth's statement that it is up to Trump whether the war is 'at the beginning, the middle, or the end' — revealing internal incoherence. Deeper obstacles remain: the expected regime collapse has not materialized, and accepting an IRGC-managed transition government — the Venezuela model Trump himself floated — risks, by his own admission, the 'worst-case scenario' of installing a more extreme power center.

III. THE QUAGMIRE QUESTION

The Ghost of Iraq and the Limits of Air Power

Critics invoke the specter of Iraq 2003. Supporters argue this conflict is fundamentally different. The real question is whether Trump can find an exit before the window closes.

The scale of U.S. military commitment is significant: two carrier strike groups plus 16 additional warships — roughly 40% of total U.S. naval power — are currently engaged in the Middle East. This represents the largest American military deployment in the region since the 2003 invasion of Iraq. Seven U.S. service members have been killed, and Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Gen. Dan Caine has warned the public to brace for further casualties as operations 'continue to scale.'

Atlantic Council strategist Melanie Hart highlights a strategic cost that extends beyond the Middle East: the deployment has already required moving some of the U.S.'s most advanced missile defense systems from the Indo-Pacific — assets that, from Beijing's perspective, directly threatened Chinese security interests. 'If the United States ends up stuck in another Middle Eastern quagmire that cedes the Indo-Pacific to China, the wins will keep coming,' Hart warns.

They didn't have a plan. They have no timeline. And because of that, they have no exit strategy. — Senator Mark Kelly (D-AZ)

The domestic political calculus is deteriorating. Roughly six in ten Americans say they do not trust Trump to make the right decisions about the use of force in Iran, according to a CNN poll. Trump — who campaigned on ending 'forever wars' — has shifted his public framing of the conflict multiple times within single speeches, oscillating between 'short-term excursion' and 'we haven't won enough.' Rising fuel prices, potential further casualties, and an unclear endpoint constitute a significant political liability heading into the 2026 midterms.

Yet an alternative scenario is equally plausible: if U.S.-Israeli strikes succeed in permanently neutralizing Iran's nuclear ambitions and ballistic missile program without requiring ground forces, and if a workable political transition emerges in Tehran, Trump could claim a historic diplomatic achievement — ending nearly five decades of U.S.-Iran confrontation. The outcome depends on two conditions neither side currently controls: a negotiated off-ramp and Iran's internal political trajectory.

IV. INTERNATIONAL DIPLOMACY: A WORLD RE-ALIGNING

The Fracture Lines of a Multipolar Crisis

The war is functioning as the most severe stress-test of the post-Cold War international order, redrawing alliances along predictable but consequential lines.

China and Russia have jointly demanded an immediate ceasefire at the UN Security Council and condemned the strikes as violations of the UN Charter. But both countries' positions contain internal tensions. Russia — which has refused comparable ceasefire calls over Ukraine — faces credibility challenges. China's calculus is more complex: Beijing has issued strong condemnations while reportedly providing Iran with electronic warfare and intelligence support, and according to some accounts, military-technical assistance in missile reconstruction. Yet analysts uniformly assess that preserving the Xi-Trump détente remains Beijing's strategic priority, with a bilateral summit expected to proceed as planned.

Among the Ukraine conflict's surprising echoes: just as Russia's invasion redrew European strategic calculations, the Iran war has pushed European allies into uncomfortable positions. NATO Secretary General Rutte endorsed the operation as targeting a 'threat.' German Chancellor Merz has been broadly supportive but publicly warned that 'more questions arise with every day of war' and that Europe 'has no interest in an endless war.' Britain and France signaled willingness for 'proportionate defensive military measures' against Iranian drones and missiles — but have carefully avoided deeper entanglement.

Turkey as Pivotal Mediator

Turkey stands as the only regional power with genuine leverage on both sides. President Erdogan — whose country Iran has conspicuously not targeted — has condemned both belligerents and announced 'intense' diplomatic efforts to bring the parties to the table. Turkey's unique position: a NATO member with historic diplomatic ties to Tehran and the only neighboring major power not drawn into the conflict's kinetic orbit.

A further destabilizing variable: Israel's simultaneous opening of a second front in Lebanon, with authorized ground incursions beginning March 3 against Hezbollah infrastructure. This expands the conflict beyond Iran proper and substantially increases the risk of a wider regional conflagration, potentially drawing in Hezbollah's fuller capacity and Lebanese state institutions.

CONCLUSION

Certainties in an Age of Uncertainty

The trajectory of this war will be defined by the answers to a small set of foundational questions.

First: will Trump accept a political outcome short of full regime change — an Iran diminished in military capability but still governed by the Islamic Republic? This is the most plausible exit, but demands domestic political courage from a president whose rhetoric has repeatedly outpaced his strategic clarity.

Second: can Iran maintain sufficient resilience to negotiate an honorable exit? The new Supreme Leader inherits a damaged but not collapsed state. His policy direction remains genuinely uncertain.

Third — and most urgently for the global economy — can the Strait of Hormuz reopen within weeks rather than months? The IEA's unprecedented 400-million-barrel reserve drawdown sends one clear signal: markets are preparing for this conflict to last far longer than initially assumed.

What is certain is this: regardless of how and when the fighting ends, the Middle East's geopolitical architecture, the structure of global energy markets, and the credibility of multilateral institutions will all bear the marks of this conflict for a generation.
